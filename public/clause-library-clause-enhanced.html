Plain-English Summary

A concise explanation of what this clause does and how it works, written in clear, non-legal language.

This clause establishes a performance measurement system using RevPAR Index (Revenue Per Available Room relative to a competitive set). The property must maintain a RevPAR Index above a specified threshold (typically 90-95%) during the operations phase. If performance falls below this threshold for consecutive periods, it triggers defined remedies including potential PIP requirements, fee adjustments, or in extreme cases, termination rights.

The competitive set is defined at contract signing and typically includes 5-8 comparable properties in the same market segment and geographic area. The RevPAR Index calculation excludes extraordinary items and uses a rolling 12-month measurement period to smooth seasonal variations.

Why It Matters

The business and financial implications of this clause for owners, brands, and other stakeholders.

Performance tests directly impact both owner and brand economics. For owners, failure to meet thresholds can result in:

  • Mandatory PIP investments with associated capital costs
  • Reduced operational flexibility due to increased brand oversight
  • Potential fee escalations or penalty payments
  • Risk of termination if performance doesn't improve

For brands, performance tests protect brand standards and ensure the property contributes positively to system-wide metrics. However, poorly negotiated thresholds can create unnecessary pressure on owners, especially during market downturns beyond their control.

The competitive set selection is critical—an improperly defined set can create unfair performance expectations. Owners should negotiate set composition, review frequency, and the right to challenge set membership during the term.

Clause Text

The actual legal language of the clause as it typically appears in agreements.

3.1 Performance Test. During the Operating Term, Owner shall maintain a RevPAR Index of not less than ninety percent (90%) measured against the Competitive Set (the "Performance Threshold"). 3.2 Calculation Methodology. RevPAR Index shall be calculated on a rolling twelve (12) month basis, excluding extraordinary items as defined in Section 8.4. The calculation shall use STR data or, if unavailable, a mutually acceptable alternative source. 3.3 Competitive Set. The Competitive Set, consisting of no fewer than five (5) and no more than eight (8) comparable properties, shall be established within sixty (60) days of Opening Date. The Competitive Set may be reviewed and adjusted annually upon mutual agreement of the Parties, provided such adjustment is commercially reasonable. 3.4 Failure to Meet Threshold. If the Property fails to meet the Performance Threshold for three (3) consecutive twelve (12) month measurement periods, Owner shall be required to implement a Performance Improvement Plan as set forth in Section 5.2. Continued failure to meet the Performance Threshold following completion of the PIP may trigger termination rights as set forth in Section 12.3. 3.5 Cure Period. Owner shall have a right to cure any Performance Threshold deficiency within ninety (90) days following the measurement period in which such deficiency occurred, provided such cure is not attributable to Market Conditions (as defined in Section 10.1).

Negotiation Notes

Practical guidance on negotiating this clause, including trade-offs, compromise options, and market considerations.

Threshold Negotiation: The 90% threshold is standard but can vary by market. In competitive markets, owners may push for 85%, while brands in strong markets may require 95%. Consider negotiating market-specific thresholds or inflation adjustments.

Competitive Set Protection: Include provisions allowing owners to challenge set membership if properties close, rebrand, or significantly renovate. Require brand to provide rationale for set inclusion and allow for independent validation.

Measurement Periods: The rolling 12-month period helps smooth seasonality. Some owners prefer shorter periods (6 months) for faster detection, while brands prefer longer periods to avoid false triggers. Consider industry-specific seasonality patterns.

Cure Provisions: The 90-day cure period is reasonable, but ensure it doesn't reset if a PIP is already in progress. Clarify that cure remedies cannot be cumulative—i.e., completing one PIP doesn't reset the failure clock if a subsequent failure occurs.

Red Flags

Warning signs and problematic language to watch for that could be unfavorable to owners.

  • No market condition carve-out: Ensure the clause excludes performance failures due to market-wide downturns, natural disasters, or force majeure events beyond owner control.
  • Competitive set locked in: Avoid clauses that prevent competitive set adjustments during the term, especially in dynamic markets where comparables change.
  • Consecutive period requirement too strict: Three consecutive periods may be too lenient for brands but too strict for owners. Consider negotiating "three out of four" or "two consecutive plus one non-consecutive" triggers.
  • PIP costs not capped: Ensure that any required PIP has reasonable cost caps and doesn't become an open-ended capital requirement.
  • No owner-initiated competitive set review: Verify that owners have the right to request competitive set reviews, not just respond to brand-initiated changes.
  • STR data availability: In markets where STR data is unavailable or unreliable, ensure alternative calculation methods are defined upfront.
